Transactional Structured Reasoning: Contamination Safety and Recovery

Abstract

We formalize a transactional execution semantics for structured reasoning with LLM-generated local proposals. The central object is not the model’s hidden chain of thought, but the committed reasoning state used to produce the final answer. We prove two basic claims. First, under sound validation, faithful rollback, and answer-extraction soundness, invalid intermediate proposals cannot contaminate committed reasoning state, and any emitted final answer is certified by a committed root certificate. Second, treating the LLM as a stochastic proposal kernel, retries and refinement can amplify the probability of eventually finding a valid local update without increasing contamination risk under exact validation, but the amount of amplification is conditional: it is the i.i.d. rate 1−(1−ph)bh1-(1-p_h)^{b_h} only under sample independence, is upper-bounded by it under shared latent failure modes, and is recovered in calibrated form via a persistence bound. These claims are intended as the reasoning-state analogue of transactional no-regression, adapted from mutable system state to mutable proof/claim state.

Setup

Let a problem instance be represented by a rooted dependency structure G=(ℋ,E,r),G = (\mathcal{H}, E, r), where ℋ\mathcal{H} is a finite set of reasoning obligations, EE is a dependency relation, and r∈ℋr \in \mathcal{H} is the root obligation whose solution determines the final answer.

The committed reasoning state at time tt is st=(Ft,Dt,Kt,σt,Πt,Bt),s_t = (F_t, D_t, K_t, \sigma_t, \Pi_t, B_t), where FtF_t is the actionable frontier, DtD_t is the set of expanded obligations, KtK_t is the set of solved obligations, σt\sigma_t is the map of committed values or claims, Πt\Pi_t is the set of certificates/provenance records, and BtB_t is the remaining retry budget.

A local proposal is an update ut∈𝒰.u_t \in \mathcal{U}. Depending on the task, utu_t may be a numeric local solution, an executable local expression, an assignment claim, an elimination claim, or a structural expansion.

The LLM is modeled as a stochastic proposal kernel: Uh,k∼πθ(⋅∣s,h,k),U_{h,k} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s, h, k), where hh is the current obligation and kk is the attempt index. The executor does not directly trust samples from πθ\pi_\theta. Every proposed update is passed through a validator g(s,u)∈{0,1}.g(s,u) \in \{0,1\}.

Definition 1 (Committed-state transition). Given state ss and proposal uu, let s⊕us \oplus u denote the candidate state obtained by applying uu to a copy of ss. The committed transition is T(s,u)={s⊕uif g(s,u)=1 and V(s⊕u)<V(s),sotherwise.T(s,u) = \begin{cases} s \oplus u & \text{if } g(s,u)=1 \text{ and } V(s \oplus u) < V(s),\\ s & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} The rejected case is a rollback: the committed state remains exactly ss.

Definition 2 (Rejection memory). The executor may also maintain an auxiliary rejection memory RtR_t containing records of rejected proposals, validator reasons, and retry counts. This is operational feedback, not committed reasoning state. It may condition later LLM proposals, but it is excluded from σt\sigma_t, Πt\Pi_t, and final answer extraction.

On a rejected proposal utu_t for obligation hth_t, the operational context may change from (st,Rt)(s_t,R_t) to (st,Rt∪{(ht,ut,reasont)}),(s_t, R_t \cup \{(h_t,u_t,\mathrm{reason}_t)\}), or to a bounded/windowed version of that update. The committed projection remains exactly sts_t. On an accepted update, RtR_t may be reset or scoped to the new committed state, because the entailment context has changed.

Definition 3 (Bad and clean states). Let 𝖡𝖺𝖽(s)=1\mathsf{Bad}(s)=1 if committed state ss contains at least one invalid committed claim, value, or certificate. Otherwise 𝖡𝖺𝖽(s)=0\mathsf{Bad}(s)=0. Define 𝖢𝗅𝖾𝖺𝗇(s)⇔𝖡𝖺𝖽(s)=0.\mathsf{Clean}(s) \iff \mathsf{Bad}(s)=0. The predicate 𝖢𝗅𝖾𝖺𝗇\mathsf{Clean} concerns committed reasoning state only. The LLM may generate invalid hidden text, invalid drafts, or invalid candidate proposals; these are not contamination unless they enter σt\sigma_t or Πt\Pi_t. Rejection memory RtR_t is also not contamination, provided final answer extraction ignores RtR_t and RtR_t is not treated as a committed fact store.

Definition 4 (Progress potential). V:𝒮→ℕV: \mathcal{S}\to \mathbb{N} is a nonnegative integer potential measuring unresolved work. In the current implementation this may be an action-unit potential such as V(s)=|{h∈ℋ:h∉Kt}|+|{h∈ℋ:Ch(h)≠∅,h∉Dt}|.V(s)= |\{h \in \mathcal{H}: h \notin K_t\}| + |\{h \in \mathcal{H}: \mathrm{Ch}(h)\neq\emptyset, h\notin D_t\}|. For logic-grid tasks, VV may instead count unresolved person obligations and remaining candidate claims.

Claim I: Contamination Safety

The first claim is the reasoning-state analogue of transactional safety: invalid intermediate thoughts cannot poison the state used for the final answer.

Assumption 1 (Initial clean state). 𝖢𝗅𝖾𝖺𝗇(s0).\mathsf{Clean}(s_0).

Assumption 2 (Exclusive commit authority). Only the transactional executor can mutate committed state fields (Dt,Kt,σt,Πt)(D_t,K_t,\sigma_t,\Pi_t). Model samples, prompts, scratchpads, and rejected candidate states are not directly committed.

Assumption 3 (Faithful rollback). If a proposal uu is rejected, the next committed state equals the previous committed state: T(s,u)=s.T(s,u)=s.

Assumption 4 (Validator soundness). For every clean state ss and proposal uu, 𝖢𝗅𝖾𝖺𝗇(s)∧g(s,u)=1⟹𝖢𝗅𝖾𝖺𝗇(s⊕u).\mathsf{Clean}(s) \wedge g(s,u)=1 \implies \mathsf{Clean}(s \oplus u). For exact validators, this is enforced by checking the proposed local update against an oracle evaluator, solver, proof checker, or entailment checker.

Assumption 5 (Final answer reads only committed state). The final answer is computed by a function A:𝒮→𝒴A : \mathcal{S}\to \mathcal{Y} that depends only on committed fields such as σt\sigma_t and Πt\Pi_t, not on rejected proposals or hidden LLM drafts.

Assumption 6 (Answer-extraction soundness). The executor emits a final answer only when the root obligation rr has been solved, i.e. r∈Ktr\in K_t, and the answer is computed deterministically from a committed certificate Πt(r)\Pi_t(r) that the same validator gg has accepted as a witness for σt(r)\sigma_t(r) being the correct value of rr. Equivalently, the validator is invoked on the root certificate before any answer leaves the executor, and accepted certificates correctly witness their claims under the validator’s soundness guarantee.

Assumption 5 is purely structural: it says rejected proposals are not read at answer time. Assumption 6 is what is needed for correctness of an emitted answer; without it, contamination safety guarantees only that the answer was extracted from a clean committed state, not that the emitted value is right.

Theorem 1 (Transactional Reasoning Contamination Safety). Assume the initial clean state, exclusive commit authority, faithful rollback, and validator soundness assumptions (Assumptions 1–4). Then every committed state reachable by the transactional executor is clean: ∀t≥0,𝖢𝗅𝖾𝖺𝗇(st).\forall t \ge 0,\quad \mathsf{Clean}(s_t). Consequently, rejected or invalid intermediate proposals cannot contaminate the reasoning state used to compute the final answer.

(Note that Assumption 5 is needed only to guarantee the final answer reads exclusively from this clean committed state, and Assumption 6 is needed only for the correctness corollary below; the cleanness of sts_t itself does not depend on either.)

Proof. The proof is by induction on tt.

Base case. By the initial clean-state assumption, 𝖢𝗅𝖾𝖺𝗇(s0)\mathsf{Clean}(s_0).

Inductive step. Assume 𝖢𝗅𝖾𝖺𝗇(st)\mathsf{Clean}(s_t). Consider a proposal utu_t. There are two cases.

If utu_t is rejected, faithful rollback gives st+1=T(st,ut)=st.s_{t+1}=T(s_t,u_t)=s_t. Therefore 𝖢𝗅𝖾𝖺𝗇(st+1)\mathsf{Clean}(s_{t+1}) by the induction hypothesis.

If utu_t is accepted, then g(st,ut)=1g(s_t,u_t)=1 and st+1=st⊕ut.s_{t+1}=s_t\oplus u_t. By validator soundness and 𝖢𝗅𝖾𝖺𝗇(st)\mathsf{Clean}(s_t), it follows that 𝖢𝗅𝖾𝖺𝗇(st⊕ut),\mathsf{Clean}(s_t\oplus u_t), and hence 𝖢𝗅𝖾𝖺𝗇(st+1)\mathsf{Clean}(s_{t+1}).

Thus 𝖢𝗅𝖾𝖺𝗇(st)\mathsf{Clean}(s_t) holds for all reachable committed states. Since the final answer function reads only committed state, rejected or invalid intermediate proposals cannot affect the final answer except by failing to contribute a valid committed update. ◻

Corollary 1 (No poisoning under exact validation). If the validator is exact, 𝖢𝗅𝖾𝖺𝗇(s0)\mathsf{Clean}(s_0), and the final answer depends only on committed state, then the final answer cannot be poisoned by invalid rejected thoughts.

Corollary 2 (Conditional correctness of emitted answers). Under Assumptions 1–6 with exact validation, if the executor emits a final answer, that answer is correct.

Proof. By Theorem 1 every committed state sts_t is clean. By Assumption 6 an answer is emitted only when r∈Ktr\in K_t and the validator has accepted the root certificate Πt(r)\Pi_t(r); exact validation then implies Πt(r)\Pi_t(r) correctly witnesses σt(r)\sigma_t(r). The emitted answer is a deterministic function of these committed fields, so it agrees with the true value of rr. ◻

Interpretation.

Theorem 1 alone does not give correctness; it gives only that whatever lands in committed state was accepted by the validator on a clean predecessor. Correctness of an emitted answer requires the additional Assumption 6: the same validator must certify the root before extraction. Even with both assumptions, the executor may still fail to emit (retry budget exhausted, stall, no valid proposal), so the guarantee is conditional on emission. The role of Theorem 1 in this picture is to ensure the predecessor state on which the root certificate is checked is itself clean, so the validator’s local guarantee composes into a global correctness guarantee.

Progress and Termination

Contamination safety prevents bad updates from poisoning the answer. A separate progress condition prevents the system from accepting valid but unhelpful updates forever.

Lemma 1 (Monotone progress). If every accepted proposal satisfies V(s⊕u)<V(s),V(s \oplus u) < V(s), and rejected proposals leave ss unchanged, then V(st)V(s_t) strictly decreases on accepted steps and never increases on rejected steps.

Proof. Accepted steps decrease VV by the commit rule. Rejected steps produce st+1=sts_{t+1}=s_t, so V(st+1)=V(st)V(s_{t+1})=V(s_t). ◻

Theorem 2 (Finite-budget termination). Assume VV is a nonnegative integer, accepted steps strictly decrease VV, and rejected steps consume finite retry budget. Then the executor terminates with success, stall, budget exhaustion, or a configured max-step cap.

Proof. There can be at most V(s0)V(s_0) accepted steps before the potential reaches zero, and at most B0B_0 rejected steps before retry budget is exhausted. A configured max-step cap gives an additional finite bound. ◻

Claim II: Recovery by Retry

Safety alone is insufficient. The useful property is that rejected local failures are non-catastrophic: after rollback, the executor can try again from the same clean state.

Fix a clean state ss and a frontier obligation hh. Define the one-shot local success probability ph(s)=PrU∼πθ(⋅∣s,h)[g(s,U)=1∧V(s⊕U)<V(s)].p_h(s) = \Pr_{U \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s,h)} \left[ g(s,U)=1 \ \wedge\ V(s\oplus U)<V(s) \right]. This is the probability that one LLM sample proposes an acceptable, progressive local update.

Theorem 3 (Retry amplification, i.i.d. regime). Suppose the executor attempts obligation hh up to bhb_h times from the same clean committed state ss, rejected attempts leave ss unchanged, and attempts are conditionally independent with identical local success probability ph(s)p_h(s). Then the probability of at least one valid commit for hh is qh(s,bh)=1−(1−ph(s))bh.q_h(s,b_h) = 1-(1-p_h(s))^{b_h}.

Proof. Each attempt fails with probability 1−ph(s)1-p_h(s). By conditional independence, all bhb_h attempts fail with probability (1−ph(s))bh.(1-p_h(s))^{b_h}. Therefore at least one attempt succeeds with probability 1−(1−ph(s))bh.1-(1-p_h(s))^{b_h}. Because rejected attempts roll back to the same clean committed state, failed samples do not alter the success probability by contaminating future attempts. ◻

Corollary 3 (Almost-sure local recovery under nonzero support). If ph(s)>0p_h(s)>0, then limbh→∞qh(s,bh)=1.\lim_{b_h\to\infty} q_h(s,b_h)=1.

Interpretation.

The LLM need not be reliable globally. It only needs to assign nonzero probability mass to valid local proposals. Transactions allow the executor to sample this distribution repeatedly while preventing invalid samples from corrupting state.

Sample Correlation and the Limits of Retry Amplification

Theorem 3 assumes that the bhb_h retries on obligation hh are conditionally independent. Empirically, repeated samples from a fixed LLM on a fixed prompt are correlated: failure modes tend to repeat. The realistic generative model has a latent “hardness” or “failure-mode” variable ZZ that is sampled once per obligation (encoding, e.g., which sub-pattern of the prompt the model is currently confusing), with attempts conditionally independent given ZZ.

Proposition 1 (Correlation makes retry amplification an upper bound). Suppose attempts on obligation hh share a latent variable ZZ such that, conditional on ZZ, the failure indicators Yk=𝟏[Xk=0]Y_k=\mathbf{1}[X_k=0] are i.i.d. Bernoulli(ρ(Z))(\rho(Z)) for some measurable ρ:Z→[0,1]\rho:Z\to[0,1] with 𝔼[ρ(Z)]=1−ph\mathbb{E}[\rho(Z)]=1-p_h. Then Pr⁡[Y1=⋯=Ybh=1]=𝔼[ρ(Z)bh]≥(𝔼[ρ(Z)])bh=(1−ph)bh,\Pr[Y_1=\cdots=Y_{b_h}=1] =\mathbb{E}\!\left[\rho(Z)^{b_h}\right] \;\ge\; \bigl(\mathbb{E}[\rho(Z)]\bigr)^{b_h} =(1-p_h)^{b_h}, by Jensen’s inequality applied to the convex function x↦xbhx\mapsto x^{b_h}. Equivalently, Pr⁡[success in bh tries]≤1−(1−ph)bh.\Pr[\text{success in } b_h \text{ tries}] \;\le\; 1-(1-p_h)^{b_h}.

The bound of Theorem 3 is therefore an upper bound on the achievable retry amplification under the latent-mode model, not a guarantee. The gap 𝔼[ρ(Z)bh]−(1−ph)bh\mathbb{E}[\rho(Z)^{b_h}]-(1-p_h)^{b_h} grows with the variance of ρ(Z)\rho(Z): when failure mode is essentially deterministic per-prompt (ρ(Z)∈{0,1}\rho(Z)\in\{0,1\} almost surely), retries achieve no amplification and the realised success rate is exactly php_h regardless of bhb_h.

Diversity restores amplification.

The framework therefore implicitly relies on diversity-inducing machinery to make samples behave more like independent draws and reduce the variance of ρ(Z)\rho(Z). Practical mechanisms include sampling temperature, prompt paraphrasing, varied few-shot exemplars, distinct draft variables, and ensembling across decoders or model instances. Each such intervention can be viewed as enlarging the support of ZZ relative to a fixed-prompt baseline.

A more conservative practical bound.

For empirical use, a pessimistic single-parameter alternative to Theorem 3 is the conditional persistence bound. Let Fk={Y1=⋯=Yk=1}F_k=\{Y_1=\cdots=Y_k=1\} be the event that the first kk attempts on obligation hh all fail, and assume the uniform persistence condition Pr⁡[Yk+1=1∣Fk]≤ρhfor all k=1,…,bh−1,\Pr[Y_{k+1}=1 \mid F_k] \;\le\; \rho_h \quad \text{for all } k=1,\dots,b_h-1, with ρh∈[1−ph,1]\rho_h\in[1-p_h,1]. This is a stronger assumption than mere one-step correlation: failures must remain at most ρh\rho_h-likely conditional on all previous failures, not only on the immediately previous one. Then, applying the chain rule, Pr⁡[Fbh]=Pr⁡[Y1=1]∏k=1bh−1Pr⁡[Yk+1=1∣Fk]≤(1−ph)ρhbh−1,\Pr[F_{b_h}] \;=\; \Pr[Y_1=1]\prod_{k=1}^{b_h-1}\Pr[Y_{k+1}=1\mid F_k] \;\le\; (1-p_h)\,\rho_h^{\,b_h-1}, so Pr⁡[success in bh tries]≥1−(1−ph)ρhbh−1.\Pr[\text{success in } b_h \text{ tries}] \;\ge\; 1 - (1-p_h)\,\rho_h^{\,b_h-1}. The bound interpolates between independence (ρh=1−ph\rho_h=1-p_h, recovering the Theorem 3 rate) and total correlation (ρh=1\rho_h=1, no amplification beyond the first attempt). The uniform persistence parameter ρh\rho_h can be calibrated from logged retry trajectories by computing, for each prefix length kk, the empirical fraction of trajectories whose (k+1)(k{+}1)-st attempt fails given FkF_k, and taking the supremum over kk.

Global Success Under Oracle Structure

Let ℛ⊆ℋ\mathcal{R} \subseteq \mathcal{H} be the set of required obligations for a successful derivation under a fixed oracle structure, topologically ordered as h1,…,hMh_1,\ldots,h_M along dependency edges. Suppose the executor must obtain one valid commit for each h∈ℛh\in\mathcal{R}. We separate the structural chain-rule argument from the local-rate model.

Theorem 4 (Product lower bound, abstract form). Assume exact validation and rollback safety. Suppose that for each obligation hih_i, conditional on all prior obligations h1,…,hi−1h_1,\ldots,h_{i-1} having been resolved (i.e. on reaching a clean committed state in which hih_i becomes the active frontier element), the executor resolves hih_i with probability at least qhiLBq_{h_i}^{\mathrm{LB}}. Then Pr⁡[success]≥∏i=1MqhiLB.\Pr[\mathrm{success}] \;\ge\; \prod_{i=1}^{M} q_{h_i}^{\mathrm{LB}}.

Proof. Let EiE_i denote the event that hih_i is resolved. By the chain rule, Pr⁡[⋂i=1MEi]=∏i=1MPr⁡[Ei|⋂j<iEj]≥∏i=1MqhiLB,\Pr\!\left[\bigcap_{i=1}^{M}E_i\right] =\prod_{i=1}^{M}\Pr\!\left[E_i\;\bigg|\;\bigcap_{j<i}E_j\right] \;\ge\; \prod_{i=1}^{M} q_{h_i}^{\mathrm{LB}}, using the assumed conditional lower bound at each topological position. Exact validation and clean predecessor states (Theorem 1) ensure the preconditions of each qhiLBq_{h_i}^{\mathrm{LB}} are satisfied along any path that reaches hih_i. ◻

The role of any specific local model — i.i.d. retries, persistence bound, exchangeable mixture — is to supply qhiLBq_{h_i}^{\mathrm{LB}}. Three natural instantiations:

Corollary 4 (Instantiation: independent retries). If, conditional on reaching the state at which hih_i is attempted, the bhib_{h_i} retries are i.i.d. with marginal success probability at least phip_{h_i}, then qhiLB=1−(1−phi)bhiq_{h_i}^{\mathrm{LB}} = 1-(1-p_{h_i})^{b_{h_i}} and Pr⁡[success]≥∏i=1M(1−(1−phi)bhi).\Pr[\mathrm{success}] \;\ge\; \prod_{i=1}^{M}\left(1-(1-p_{h_i})^{b_{h_i}}\right).

Corollary 5 (Instantiation: persistence-calibrated retries). If, conditional on reaching the state at which hih_i is attempted, retries on hih_i satisfy the uniform persistence condition with parameter ρhi∈[1−phi,1]\rho_{h_i}\in[1-p_{h_i},1], then qhiLB=1−(1−phi)ρhibhi−1q_{h_i}^{\mathrm{LB}} = 1 - (1-p_{h_i})\rho_{h_i}^{\,b_{h_i}-1} and Pr⁡[success]≥∏i=1M(1−(1−phi)ρhibhi−1).\Pr[\mathrm{success}] \;\ge\; \prod_{i=1}^{M}\left(1-(1-p_{h_i})\,\rho_{h_i}^{\,b_{h_i}-1}\right).

Corollary 6 (Instantiation: deterministic per-prompt failure). If on each obligation the failure mode is essentially deterministic given the prompt (ρ(Z)∈{0,1}\rho(Z)\in\{0,1\} a.s. in the latent-mode model of Proposition 1), then qhiLB=phiq_{h_i}^{\mathrm{LB}} = p_{h_i} regardless of bhib_{h_i}, and the lower bound degenerates to the one-shot structured baseline ∏iphi\prod_i p_{h_i}.

Corollary 7 (Uniform i.i.d. lower bound). If, in addition to the i.i.d. instantiation, ph≥pmin>0p_h \ge p_{\min}>0 and bh≥bb_h\ge b for all h∈ℛh\in\mathcal{R}, and |ℛ|=M|\mathcal{R}|=M, then Pr⁡[success]≥(1−(1−pmin)b)M.\Pr[\mathrm{success}] \;\ge\; \left(1-(1-p_{\min})^b\right)^M.

Comparison to non-transactional structured execution.

A one-shot structured baseline that commits the first local proposal succeeds with probability approximately ∏h∈ℛph\prod_{h\in\mathcal{R}} p_h (and is also unsafe: rejected proposals can corrupt downstream state). Transactional retry changes the local factor from php_h to qhLBq_h^{\mathrm{LB}}, where qhLB∈[ph,1−(1−ph)bh]q_h^{\mathrm{LB}}\in[p_h,\, 1-(1-p_h)^{b_h}] depending on the diversity of samples within an obligation. Exact validation simultaneously prevents failed attempts from contaminating downstream obligations.

Recovery by Refinement

Retry is not the only recovery operator. If an obligation is too coarse, the executor can refine it into smaller obligations.

Definition 5 (Refinement). A refinement operator replaces a failed obligation hh with child obligations 𝖱𝖾𝖿𝗂𝗇𝖾(h)={c1,…,cm}\mathsf{Refine}(h)=\{c_1,\ldots,c_m\} plus, optionally, a recomposition obligation that combines the children into a solution for hh.

Let qhq_h denote the (calibrated or assumed) probability that the executor resolves hh directly under its allocated retry budget — that is, the qhLBq_h^{\mathrm{LB}} supplied by whichever local-rate model (Corollaries 4–6) applies. Let qciq_{c_i} denote the analogous resolution probability for child cic_i. Let qexpq_{\mathrm{exp}} be the probability that the refinement step itself is proposed validly and accepted by the validator, and let qcompq_{\mathrm{comp}} be the probability that the children can be recomposed into a validated solution for hh.

Proposition 2 (When refinement improves recovery). Suppose that, conditional on refinement being accepted, the events “cic_i resolved” for i=1,…,mi=1,\ldots,m and “recomposition succeeds” are mutually independent — a substantive assumption, equivalent to asserting that no shared latent failure mode couples the children. Then refinement improves the probability of resolving hh whenever qexp(∏i=1mqci)qcomp>qh.q_{\mathrm{exp}} \left(\prod_{i=1}^m q_{c_i}\right) q_{\mathrm{comp}} \;>\; q_h. Without the cross-child independence assumption, the same comparison holds with ∏i=1mqci\prod_{i=1}^m q_{c_i} replaced by the joint resolution probability Pr⁡[all children resolved∣refinement accepted]\Pr[\text{all children resolved}\mid \text{refinement accepted}], which must be supplied by the local-rate model.

Proof. The left-hand side is the probability that refinement is accepted, every child obligation is resolved, and recomposition succeeds, assuming the stated factorisation across children. The right-hand side is the calibrated probability of resolving hh without refinement. Refinement is beneficial exactly when the former exceeds the latter. The fall-back form follows by replacing the product with the joint probability and applying the same inequality. ◻

Interpretation.

This is a decision rule for adaptive decomposition under whichever model of local resolution the system has calibrated. When local rates are php_h and samples are i.i.d., qhq_h takes the form 1−(1−ph)bh1-(1-p_h)^{b_h} and the comparison reduces to the original retry-vs-refinement formula. When samples are correlated, qhq_h may be much smaller than this i.i.d. value, and the comparison should use the calibrated qhq_h rather than substituting the i.i.d. formula. Naively using the i.i.d. amplified rate when samples share a latent failure mode tends to make refinement look worse than it is, since diverse children can break a shared failure mode that a single coarser obligation cannot.

Noisy Validators

Exact validators are the cleanest case. For freeform reasoning, validators may be noisy. Two distinct false-accept rates that are easy to confuse must be kept separate: αh→=Pr⁡[g(s,U)=1∣U is invalid](conditional false-accept rate),\alpha_h^{\rightarrow} = \Pr[g(s,U)=1 \mid U \text{ is invalid}] \quad\text{(conditional false-accept rate),} αh←=Pr⁡[U is invalid∣g(s,U)=1](posterior false-accept rate),\alpha_h^{\leftarrow} = \Pr[U \text{ is invalid} \mid g(s,U)=1] \quad\text{(posterior false-accept rate),} βh=Pr⁡[g(s,U)=0∣U is valid](false-reject rate).\beta_h = \Pr[g(s,U)=0 \mid U \text{ is valid}] \quad\text{(false-reject rate).} The two false-accept rates are related by Bayes, αh←=αh→Pr⁡[U invalid]αh→Pr⁡[U invalid]+(1−βh)Pr⁡[U valid],\alpha_h^{\leftarrow} = \frac{\alpha_h^{\rightarrow}\,\Pr[U \text{ invalid}]} {\alpha_h^{\rightarrow}\,\Pr[U \text{ invalid}] +(1-\beta_h)\,\Pr[U \text{ valid}]}, and αh←\alpha_h^{\leftarrow} can be much larger than αh→\alpha_h^{\rightarrow} when the proposal kernel is weak (high Pr⁡[U invalid]\Pr[U\text{ invalid}]) or the validator is conservative on valid proposals (high βh\beta_h). The two yield genuinely different contamination bounds.

Theorem 5 (Contamination bound, per-invocation form). Let NN be the (random) total number of validator invocations during a run, with deterministic upper bound N‾\bar{N} given by retry budget plus the commit cap. If every validator invocation has conditional false-accept rate at most αmax⁡→\alpha_{\max}^{\rightarrow}, then Pr⁡[∃ bad committed update]≤N‾αmax⁡→.\Pr[\exists \text{ bad committed update}] \;\le\; \bar{N}\,\alpha_{\max}^{\rightarrow}.

Proof. Index validator invocations by j=1,…,Nj=1,\dots,N. Let Vj=𝟏[gj=1]V_j=\mathbf{1}[g_j=1] and Ij=𝟏[Uj invalid]I_j=\mathbf{1}[U_j \text{ invalid}]. A bad commit at invocation jj is the event Vj=1∧Ij=1V_j=1\wedge I_j=1, with Pr⁡[Vj=1,Ij=1]=Pr⁡[Vj=1∣Ij=1]Pr⁡[Ij=1]≤αmax⁡→⋅1.\Pr[V_j=1, I_j=1] =\Pr[V_j=1\mid I_j=1]\,\Pr[I_j=1] \le \alpha_{\max}^{\rightarrow}\cdot 1. Union-bounding over the at most N‾\bar{N} invocations, Pr⁡[⋃j=1N{Vj=1,Ij=1}]≤∑j=1N‾Pr⁡[Vj=1,Ij=1]≤N‾αmax⁡→.\Pr\!\left[\bigcup_{j=1}^{N}\{V_j=1, I_j=1\}\right] \le \sum_{j=1}^{\bar{N}}\Pr[V_j=1, I_j=1] \le \bar{N}\,\alpha_{\max}^{\rightarrow}. ◻

Theorem 6 (Contamination bound, per-commit form). If the executor produces at most KK accepted commits and every accepted commit satisfies Pr⁡[U invalid∣g(s,U)=1]≤αmax⁡←\Pr[U\text{ invalid}\mid g(s,U)=1]\le\alpha_{\max}^{\leftarrow}, then Pr⁡[∃ bad committed update]≤Kαmax⁡←.\Pr[\exists \text{ bad committed update}] \;\le\;K\,\alpha_{\max}^{\leftarrow}.

Proof. For commit i∈{1,…,K}i\in\{1,\dots,K\} let BiB_i be the event that commit ii is invalid. By assumption Pr⁡[Bi]≤αmax⁡←\Pr[B_i]\le\alpha_{\max}^{\leftarrow}. Union-bounding, Pr⁡[⋃i=1KBi]≤Kαmax⁡←\Pr[\bigcup_{i=1}^{K}B_i]\le K\,\alpha_{\max}^{\leftarrow}. ◻

Why the distinction matters.

A naive union bound of the form Kαmax⁡→K\alpha_{\max}^{\rightarrow}, mixing a commit count with a conditional false-accept rate, is incorrect. As a counterexample, suppose all proposals are invalid (Pr⁡[U invalid]=1\Pr[U \text{ invalid}]=1), α→=10−3\alpha^{\rightarrow}=10^{-3}, the retry budget allows 10410^4 invocations, and the executor halts after K=1K=1 accept. Every accept is then a bad commit, and Pr⁡[∃ bad commit]=1−(1−10−3)104≈1,\Pr[\exists \text{ bad commit}] =1-(1-10^{-3})^{10^4}\approx 1, which exceeds Kα→=10−3K\alpha^{\rightarrow}=10^{-3} by three orders of magnitude. Theorem 5 gives the correct value 104⋅10−3=1010^4\cdot 10^{-3}=10, which is vacuous but not false; this reflects that the operating regime is genuinely unsafe.

Which bound to use.

The conditional rate αh→\alpha_h^{\rightarrow} is what one usually estimates when calibrating a learned validator (held-out invalid examples passed through the validator). The per-invocation bound is therefore the directly measurable one, at the cost of paying for every rejected attempt. The per-commit bound is tighter when it applies, but αh←\alpha_h^{\leftarrow} depends on the proposal distribution and is not a property of the validator alone.

Effect of false rejects on recovery.

False rejects do not contaminate state, but they reduce recovery probability. If the model proposes a valid local update with probability php_h and the validator rejects valid proposals with probability βh\beta_h, then the effective accepted local success probability is ph′=ph(1−βh),p'_h = p_h(1-\beta_h), and retry amplification becomes qh′=1−(1−ph(1−βh))bh.q'_h = 1-(1-p_h(1-\beta_h))^{b_h}.

Open Problems

The conditional guarantees above leave the substantive design problems open. We collect them here.

Constructing sound validators.

Theorem 1 assumes a sound validator. For most freeform reasoning targets (natural-language proofs, multi-step plans with side effects, open-ended program synthesis), constructing such a validator is the central problem. The framework is most directly useful for tasks that admit a cheap, exact local checker: numeric solvers, executable code with deterministic oracles, formal proofs in a checkable calculus, and constraint problems with verifier subroutines. Identifying the boundary of validator-friendly tasks, and designing decompositions that push freeform problems into validator- friendly sub-obligations, are the substantive research questions.

Optimal budget allocation across obligations.

Given a total invocation budget BB and required obligations ℛ={h1,…,hM}\mathcal{R}=\{h_1,\dots,h_M\} with estimated local success rates p̂1,…,p̂M\hat p_1,\dots,\hat p_M, the natural objective derived from the product lower bound is maxb1,…,bM≥0∑i=1Mlog⁡(1−(1−p̂i)bi)s.t.∑i=1Mbi≤B.\max_{b_1,\dots,b_M\ge 0} \; \sum_{i=1}^{M}\log\!\left(1-(1-\hat p_i)^{b_i}\right) \quad\text{s.t.}\quad \sum_{i=1}^{M} b_i \le B. This is a separable concave allocation; its KKT conditions yield a water-filling rule (1−p̂i)bilog⁡11−p̂i1−(1−p̂i)bi=λ∀i∈ℛ,\frac{(1-\hat p_i)^{b_i}\log\frac{1}{1-\hat p_i}} {1-(1-\hat p_i)^{b_i}} \;=\; \lambda \quad\forall i\in\mathcal{R}, for a common dual λ\lambda tuned to the budget. With unknown p̂i\hat p_i, the problem becomes a structured multi-armed bandit: each obligation is a Bernoulli arm with unknown success probability, retries are pulls, but pulls also drain a shared budget and feed into a topological dependency. Whether standard strategies (UCB, Thompson sampling, or an explore-then-allocate phase based on the water-filling rule above) admit useful regret guarantees against the offline product optimum is open.

Adaptive refinement policies.

Proposition 2 compares “solve hh directly” against “refine hh then solve its children” as if they were exclusive. In practice, the executor first attempts hh for some number of tries and refines only on persistent failure. The right object is a sequential policy that, after kk failed attempts on hh, decides among continue, refine, or abandon, given a posterior over php_h and the remaining budget. Casting this as an optimal stopping problem and characterising the resulting policy (presumably a threshold rule on the posterior mean of php_h relative to the per-child rates) is open.

Strengthening the contamination bound under correlated validator errors.

The per-invocation union bound N‾αmax⁡→\bar N\,\alpha_{\max}^{\rightarrow} of Theorem 5 is loose when validator false accepts are correlated across invocations, e.g. a learned validator with a systematic blind spot for a particular invalid pattern. In that regime the right object is a mixture model over latent “vulnerability classes” that the validator either does or does not catch, with concentration measured against the structure of the proposal distribution rather than the number of attempts. We do not address this here.

Joint optimisation of validator and proposer.

The framework treats πθ\pi_\theta and gg as independent. In practice they share a model family, and improving the validator can degrade the proposer (and vice versa) under a fixed compute budget. The Pareto frontier between php_h, αh→\alpha_h^{\rightarrow}, and βh\beta_h at fixed compute is the practically relevant object, and is unaddressed.

Quantifying the latent-mode variance.

Proposition 1 reduces the gap between realised and ideal retry amplification to the variance of ρ(Z)\rho(Z). Empirical calibration of this variance for representative model/prompt families, together with a quantitative theory of how diversity-inducing interventions shrink it, would convert Theorem 3 from an asymptotic target into a usable operating bound.

Summary of Claims

The transactional structured reasoning framework supports two core theoretical claims, one structural and one stochastic.

Contamination safety and conditional correctness.

Under clean initialization, exclusive commit authority, faithful rollback, sound validation, final-answer dependence only on committed state, and answer-extraction soundness, two things follow:

  1. every committed state reachable by the executor is clean (Theorem 1);

  2. if the executor emits a final answer, that answer is correct (Corollary 2).

Contamination safety is the structural part — bad samples don’t enter committed state — and is unconditional given the assumptions. Correctness of an emitted answer additionally requires that the validator certify the root certificate before extraction. Neither result claims the executor will emit; failure modes (timeout, stall, budget exhaustion) remain.

Safe retry plus conditional recovery improvement.

Treating the LLM as a stochastic proposal kernel, transactional retries under exact validation cannot increase contamination risk relative to a one-shot baseline (rejected attempts roll back to a clean state). The quantitative improvement they provide depends on a local-rate model:

  • under conditional independence, the per-obligation rate amplifies from php_h to 1−(1−ph)bh1-(1-p_h)^{b_h} (Theorem 3);

  • under shared latent failure modes, that i.i.d. rate is an upper bound on what is achievable, and the realised rate can be as low as php_h (Proposition 1);

  • under a calibrated uniform persistence parameter ρh\rho_h, the rate is at least 1−(1−ph)ρhbh−11-(1-p_h)\rho_h^{\,b_h-1}, interpolating between the two extremes.

The global product lower bound (Theorem 4) is stated abstractly in terms of per-obligation lower bounds qhLBq_h^{\mathrm{LB}}, which the local-rate model supplies. Refinement is beneficial when the analogous expression for refinement exceeds qhq_h for the same calibrated rates.

Scope.

These are conditional guarantees, not claims that arbitrary long-horizon reasoning is solved. The structural guarantees (no contamination, conditional correctness) hold whenever the assumptions hold. The recovery improvement is guaranteed to be safe but its magnitude is a function of validator strength, local proposal quality, and sample diversity — none of which the framework itself produces. The substantive engineering questions (constructing sound validators for the task class, inducing enough sample diversity to push ρh\rho_h below 1, allocating retry budget across obligations) remain open and are surveyed in Section 9.
